Sentinel.
LatAm Civil-Military Monitor

Latin America is in the middle of a structural contest over who controls the gun. Across the region, the boundary between civilian government and military institution — hard-won through the democratic transitions of the 1980s and 1990s — is under pressure from multiple directions at once. In El Salvador, a president has turned the armed forces into the operational core of a permanent state of exception. In Mexico, the military now commands a national police force enshrined in the constitution. In Bolivia, an armored column rammed the doors of the presidential palace in 2024. In Brazil, coup-planning documents were found in the homes of senior officers after January 8. And in Venezuela, the FANB has become the last institutional pillar holding a collapsing regime together.

These are not isolated episodes. They reflect a regional pattern: civilian control of the military, where it exists, is increasingly conditional — contingent on political alignment, economic crisis, and the willingness of individual commanders to hold the line. SENTINEL tracks this pattern in near-real time, country by country, event by event. The question driving the project is not whether coups happen, but whether the norms and institutions that make them costly are eroding — and where the erosion is fastest.

Four special monitors provide deep analytical coverage of the region's most consequential cases: Venezuela (post-Maduro transition), Colombia (Total Peace doctrine and the FARC's JEP reckoning), El Salvador (Bukele's institutionalized exception), and Mexico (structural militarization under Sheinbaum). US Security Cooperation
US security cooperation is a central variable in Latin American CMR: it shapes military doctrine, equipment, training culture, and — in some cases — institutional loyalty. The Greenbook tracks US foreign aid obligations back to 1946 in constant dollars. Current security programs (FMF, IMET, INCLE, Section 333, NADR) represent an estimated $1.8B annually to the region in FY2025. This tab documents historical flows, active programs, US military installations, and the dramatic operational expansion since January 2025 — including the most consequential US military action in the hemisphere since Panama 1989.
★ Special Focus US Military & Security Operations in the Caribbean — 2025–26
The Trump administration has pursued an assertive Caribbean security posture since January 2025, combining direct military action (Operation Absolute Resolve, Jan 3, 2026), institutional pressure on Haiti's security transition, expanded counter-narcotics deployments, and a strategic realignment of bilateral relationships that has reshaped SOUTHCOM's regional footprint. The Caribbean — long treated as a secondary theatre beneath the Andean and Central American drug corridors — has become the hemisphere's most active zone of direct US military engagement. These operations mark the most consequential US military activity in the Caribbean since the 1994 Haiti intervention.

Security Civil-Military Relations Political Stability
The 2025–26 Caribbean operations represent a structural shift in US hemispheric posture — from the post-Cold War model of institution-building, aid conditionality, and multilateral frameworks toward direct coercive action and transactional bilateral deals. Operation Absolute Resolve sets a precedent with no post-1989 equivalent: a US head-of-state capture operation against a sovereign government in the hemisphere. The CMR implications are significant: (1) the FANB now understands that military loyalty to a sanctioned leader does not guarantee sovereignty protection; (2) smaller Caribbean militaries are recalibrating threat assessments to include the possibility of US direct action; (3) the CECOT deportation architecture shows US willingness to co-finance detention systems that bypass OAS and Inter-American human rights frameworks. SENTINEL assesses the long-term effect on regional civil-military norms as destabilizing — the intervention may reduce authoritarian durability in the short run while normalizing extralegal executive action.

Section 333 (formerly 1206) enables the DoD to directly train and equip foreign military partners without State Dept. co-lead.

The country simultaneously pursues negotiated peace processes with multiple armed actors while sustaining one of the region's highest-intensity internal conflicts. U.S. security cooperation — over $461M annually — intersects with Colombia's DDR programs, coca eradication strategy, and ongoing FARC-dissident operations. The Petro government's "Total Peace" doctrine marks a significant departure from prior administrations and carries significant implications for civil-military relations, military doctrine, and U.S. partnership.

⚠ Critical Development — Jan 3, 2026
US forces conducted Operation Absolute Resolve on January 3, 2026, seizing President Maduro in Caracas. He was arraigned on narco-terrorism charges in New York on January 5. Vice President Delcy Rodríguez is now acting president. Venezuela entered an unprecedented post-authoritarian transition on January 3, 2026 when US special operations forces, acting on the 2020 narco-terrorism indictment, captured Nicolás Maduro in his Caracas compound in Operation Absolute Resolve. Maduro and Cilia Flores were arraigned in New York on January 5. Vice President Delcy Rodríguez was sworn in as acting president under constitutional succession; she may hold the position for up to six months before elections must be called. The FANB — whose 2,000+ generals and parallel coercive structures (SEBIN, DGCIM, colectivos) were built as a personalist loyalty system around Maduro — now faces an acute succession and loyalty crisis. Defense Minister Padrino López issued a loyalty statement backing Rodríguez, while DGCIM simultaneously detained at least 12 officers suspected of facilitating the operation. The opposition and international community are pressing for free elections; the status of Edmundo González Urrutia's claim to the presidency remains contested. SENTINEL is tracking FANB cohesion, Rodríguez's authority, election timeline, and sanctions trajectory.

El Salvador
Special Monitor
Constitutional overhaul — July 31, 2025: The Bukele-controlled Legislative Assembly approved indefinite presidential re-election (57–3 vote), extended the presidential term from 5 to 6 years, abolished runoff elections, and moved the next presidential election to 2027. El Salvador's constitutional order has been fundamentally restructured to enable permanent executive rule. — NPR ↗ · ConstitutionNet ↗
El Salvador is Latin America's clearest active case of civil-military fusion through a populist security doctrine. President Nayib Bukele's Régimen de Excepción — declared March 27, 2022 and extended 48 consecutive times through March 2026 — suspended the right to legal defense and inviolability of communications, deploying the military alongside police in the region's largest peacetime mass incarceration campaign. Over 91,300 people have been detained; an international panel of experts (GIPES) presented findings to the UN and Inter-American Commission on Human Rights documenting at least 504 in-custody deaths and conditions meeting the threshold of crimes against humanity. The MS-13 and Barrio 18 gangs have been operationally dismantled at street level, driving the homicide rate to 1.1 per 100,000 in 2025 (82 total murders) — though a May 2025 PNC intelligence report shows 17,500+ gang members still at large. The US–El Salvador relationship has shifted from friction over democratic backsliding to deep strategic alignment under Trump: El Salvador now receives non-Salvadoran deportees directly into CECOT for $6M per year. In May 2025, the military was deployed to suppress peaceful protests — the first post-civil war use of the armed forces against civil society.

Analysis
Civil-Military RelationsPolitical Stability
El Salvador represents a textbook case of what scholars term democratic backsliding through civil-military fusion. Unlike classical coups, Bukele's consolidation proceeds via managed elections — enabled by judicial packing that reversed constitutional term limits — while using the military as a visible loyalty instrument and domestic enforcement mechanism. The February 2020 Legislative Assembly occupation was the defining inflection point: the officer corps' decision not to enforce constitutional limits on the executive signaled its institutional capture. Subsequent expansions — régimen enforcement, prison operations, protest suppression, and the July 2025 constitutional overhaul — follow this logic. The Trump alignment has effectively closed the last external accountability lever: the US not only declines to condition aid on democratic governance but actively co-finances the detention apparatus. SENTINEL classifies El Salvador as a Crisis / Civil-Military Fusion case, distinct from classical authoritarianism in maintaining electoral performance while comprehensively dismantling substantive civilian oversight of the armed forces.

Mexico
Special Monitor
Mexico is the region's most consequential case of structural militarization under civilian government — and the principal nexus between cartel wars, democratic civil-military relations, and US security pressure. The Sheinbaum administration (took office June 2024) has deepened the AMLO-era militarization model: in July 2025, SEDENA was formally granted national security intelligence authority by legislative reform, and the National Guard — constitutionally a civilian police force, built from military personnel — was transferred to SEDENA command, leaving Mexico without a nationwide civilian police institution. Military institutions now control AIFA airport, major ports, customs, the Tren Maya railway, and infrastructure contracts worth hundreds of billions. On the security front, the Sinaloa Cartel's internal fracture — triggered by the September 2024 handover of "El Mayo" Zambada to US custody — produced 2,197 homicides and 2,133 kidnappings in Sinaloa state alone. By early 2026, La Mayiza faction had reclaimed ~90% of contested territory. Analysis
Civil-Military RelationsSecurity
Mexico presents the region's most complex civil-military case: deep structural militarization under a formally democratic civilian government that has not experienced a coup or military veto. The officer corps has not asserted political preferences but has been the consistent beneficiary of institutional expansion — from the Calderón drug war to Sheinbaum's intelligence mandate. CMR theorists distinguish between military prerogatives (institutional autonomy, mission expansion) and civilian control (who holds formal authority). Mexico scores poorly on the former while maintaining formal civilian authority: Sheinbaum gives orders, but the military's economic empire, intelligence authority, and domestic security mandate are now structurally beyond reversal by any single government. The FTO designations have introduced a new variable: US sovereign pressure to allow unilateral operations on Mexican territory. Sheinbaum's consistent refusal is a civilian control success in one dimension, even as domestic militarization deepens. SENTINEL classifies Mexico as Strained — civilian control formally intact but institutional military autonomy expanding at a structurally irreversible pace.

Organized Crime, Illicit Economies & Civil-Military Relations
In Latin America, the boundaries between organized crime, illicit economies, and civil-military relations are structurally entangled — not episodic. Cocaine remains the hemisphere's dominant illicit commodity, with production at or near record levels: Colombia produced an estimated 2,664 metric tons in 2023 (UNODC), and trafficking routes through Central America, the Caribbean, and Pacific corridors drive military deployments, corruption, and cartel–state conflict across at least 14 SENTINEL-tracked countries. Fentanyl — almost entirely manufactured in Mexico using Chinese-sourced precursor chemicals — has supplanted heroin as the principal US opioid and become the central driver of FTO designations and US-Mexico security friction. Illicit mining (gold, coltan) funds armed groups in Colombia, Venezuela, Ecuador, Peru, and Brazil, directly implicating military units in protection rackets and territorial control. Transnational gangs — particularly Tren de Aragua and MS-13 — have expanded criminal operations across borders, blurring the line between domestic gang suppression and transnational security. Military institutions across the region have been progressively deputized for domestic law enforcement, with Ecuador's internal armed conflict declaration (2024) and Mexico's SEDENA intelligence mandate (2025) as the most recent structural inflection points.

Criminal infiltration of security forces represents the most acute long-term threat to civil-military relations integrity across the region. About Sentinel.
A Latin American Civil-Military Events Monitor
Project Goals

SENTINEL is an open-source, quasi-automated monitoring platform designed to support academic research on civil-military relations across 25 Latin American and Caribbean countries. The project addresses a persistent gap in the research landscape: the absence of systematically organized, near-real-time event data for scholars working across multiple country cases or tracking regional trends in democratic civil-military relations, transnational organized crime, and US security cooperation.

The platform ingests and classifies open-source reporting on a continuous basis, tracking coup attempts and plots, officer purges and command reshuffles, military deployments in domestic security, security sector reforms, US security cooperation, organized crime–military intersections, illicit economies, and armed conflict involving state security forces. High-salience events receive AI-generated analysis grounded in CMR theory, designed to prompt scholarly interpretation rather than replace it.

The dashboard comprises eight functional tabs: Overview (choropleth map + dashboard summary), Events (live event feed with Leaflet map), Country Profiles (25 countries, positions, CMR status, elections — including four special deep-dive monitors), Transnational Security (organized crime, drug trafficking, illicit economies, cartel–state dynamics), US Cooperation (USAID Greenbook historical aid, current programs, live events), Procurement & Arms (deals, supplier trends, SIPRI TIV), Timeline (chronological event feed with country activity), and About (this page).

SENTINEL is intended as a first-pass monitoring and alerting system, not a substitute for primary source verification. It is fully open-source, runs on GitHub Pages and the Anthropic API, and has no dedicated servers or databases.

Theoretical Framework

SENTINEL's classification system is grounded in comparative civil-military relations (CMR) theory. The event taxonomy reflects key concepts in the literature on democratic civilian control, military institutional autonomy, and authoritarian backsliding. Events are coded according to their theoretical significance for the following research questions:

  • Civilian control: Events that signal shifts in the balance of authority between elected civilians and military institutions — including officer dismissals, budget authority disputes, and doctrinal changes.
  • Coup-proofing: Mechanisms used by incumbents to forestall military defection — including loyalty promotions, parallel security forces, and economic co-optation of the officer corps.
  • Institutional autonomy: Indicators of military prerogatives beyond civilian purview — tutelary roles, constitutional mandates, and veto powers over security policy.
  • Security sector reform (SSR): Legislative, doctrinal, and organizational changes to armed forces — including DDR programs, defense white papers, and interoperability agreements.
  • Democratic backsliding: Patterns of institutional erosion — including the use of military force against domestic opponents, suspension of constitutional guarantees, and concentration of executive power via security decrees.
  • U.S. security cooperation: Channels and conduits of U.S. military engagement — FMF, IMET, SOUTHCOM exercises, equipment transfers, and joint operations — and their effects on recipient civil-military relations.
  • Transnational organized crime: The intersection of cartel, gang, and criminal network activity with state security forces — including counter-narcotics deployments, criminal infiltration of military and police units, illicit economy rents (drugs, mining, extortion), and state capture dynamics. SENTINEL treats military roles in the drug economy as core CMR phenomena, not peripheral security events.
Data Pipeline & Methodology

Events are ingested nightly via GitHub Actions from four source types: curated RSS feeds (InSight Crime, Reuters Latin America, NACLA, SOUTHCOM), the GDELT v2 Document API, NewsAPI, and the ACLED API (Central America, South America, Caribbean). Raw articles are filtered by a keyword list before classification, reducing API cost and irrelevant noise.

Classification is performed by Claude Haiku (Anthropic) in batches of eight articles. Each article is assigned an event type, country, salience rating (high / medium / low), and source confidence level (green / yellow / red). A second AI pass performs semantic deduplication: articles from different outlets covering the same incident are merged into a single canonical record with all sources attributed.

High-salience events receive a brief AI-generated CMR analysis — two to three sentences grounded in the theoretical framework above. The pipeline writes all events to a single JSON file, which the dashboard reads at page load. No database or backend server is involved.
