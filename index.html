★ Special Focus US Military & Security Operations in the Caribbean — 2025–26
The Trump administration has pursued an assertive Caribbean security posture since January 2025, combining direct military action (Operation Absolute Resolve, Jan 3, 2026), institutional pressure on Haiti's security transition, expanded counter-narcotics deployments, and a strategic realignment of bilateral relationships that has reshaped SOUTHCOM's regional footprint. The Caribbean — long treated as a secondary theatre beneath the Andean and Central American drug corridors — has become the hemisphere's most active zone of direct US military engagement. These operations mark the most consequential US military activity in the Caribbean since the 1994 Haiti intervention.

Security Military Political
The 2025–26 Caribbean operations represent a structural shift in US hemispheric posture — from the post-Cold War model of institution-building, aid conditionality, and multilateral frameworks toward direct coercive action and transactional bilateral deals. Operation Absolute Resolve sets a precedent with no post-1989 equivalent: a US head-of-state capture operation against a sovereign government in the hemisphere. The CMR implications are significant: (1) the FANB now understands that military loyalty to a sanctioned leader does not guarantee sovereignty protection; (2) smaller Caribbean militaries are recalibrating threat assessments to include the possibility of US direct action; (3) the CECOT deportation architecture shows US willingness to co-finance detention systems that bypass OAS and Inter-American human rights frameworks. SENTINEL assesses the long-term effect on regional civil-military norms as destabilizing — the intervention may reduce authoritarian durability in the short run while normalizing extralegal executive action.

Organized Crime, Illicit Economies & Political Order
In Latin America, the boundaries between organized crime, illicit economies, and civil-military relations are structurally entangled — not episodic. Cocaine remains the hemisphere's dominant illicit commodity, with production at or near record levels: Colombia produced an estimated 2,664 metric tons in 2023 (UNODC), and trafficking routes through Central America, the Caribbean, and Pacific corridors drive military deployments, corruption, and cartel–state conflict across at least 14 SENTINEL-tracked countries. Fentanyl — almost entirely manufactured in Mexico using Chinese-sourced precursor chemicals — has supplanted heroin as the principal US opioid and become the central driver of FTO designations and US-Mexico security friction. Illicit mining (gold, coltan) funds armed groups in Colombia, Venezuela, Ecuador, Peru, and Brazil, directly implicating military units in protection rackets and territorial control. Transnational gangs — particularly Tren de Aragua and MS-13 — have expanded criminal operations across borders, blurring the line between domestic gang suppression and transnational security. Military institutions across the region have been progressively deputized for domestic law enforcement, with Ecuador's internal armed conflict declaration (2024) and Mexico's SEDENA intelligence mandate (2025) as the most recent structural inflection points.

State Capture & Security-Force Corruption Indicators
Criminal infiltration of security forces represents the most acute long-term threat to civil-military relations integrity across the region. In Honduras, former President Hernández is serving 45 years in the US for trafficking facilitated by state security apparatus. In Guatemala, military intelligence networks (D-2) have been documented collaborating with trafficking organizations. In Mexico, systemic DEA-documented collusion at the state police and municipal levels continues despite federal military substitution. In Venezuela (pre-January 2026), FANB generals held documented interests in colectivo and Tren de Aragua criminal economies. In Haiti, MSS gang leader Jimmy "Barbecue" Chérizier has established territorial control over more than 80% of Port-au-Prince's key corridors, effectively displacing state security structures. These cases are not aberrations — they represent structurally predictable outcomes when military institutions are deployed in high-rent illicit economies without civilian oversight mechanisms.

About Sentinel.
A Latin American Civil-Military Events Monitor
Project Goals

SENTINEL is an AI-first research platform for tracking military, political, security, international, and economic risk across Latin America and the Caribbean. It is designed to help researchers, journalists, and policy users monitor how state institutions, non-state actors, executives, and external partners interact across the region.

The platform combines automated event collection, normalization, duplicate detection, actor coding, QA, and AI-generated analysis with a lighter human oversight layer. The public dashboard surfaces public-safe event records and regional monitors; the analyst console is a separate private workflow for corroboration, correction, duplicate resolution, and publication control.

SENTINEL tracks coups and coup attempts, officer purges, military deployments in domestic security, organized crime–security interactions, protest repression, reforms, peace processes, and U.S. security cooperation. It also maintains structured provenance so users can see how an event moved from ingestion to publication.

The platform is intended as a monitoring and research tool, not as a substitute for direct source verification. AI assists with the heavy lifting, but human review remains important for low-confidence, high-stakes, or analytically sensitive cases.

Research Lens

SENTINEL's event logic is grounded in comparative civil-military relations, democratic erosion, and regional security research. The system no longer relies on a single legacy event ontology alone; instead, it uses a project-specific knowledge base, event taxonomy, actor hierarchy, and AI analyst guidance designed around the region's contemporary security landscape.

That knowledge base organizes events around questions such as who is exercising coercive power, what role military institutions are playing, how state and non-state actors interact, and whether the event expands or constrains civilian control.

  • Civilian control: Events that signal shifts in the balance of authority between elected civilians and military institutions — including officer dismissals, budget authority disputes, and doctrinal changes.
  • Coup-proofing: Mechanisms used by incumbents to forestall military defection — including loyalty promotions, parallel security forces, and economic co-optation of the officer corps.
  • Institutional autonomy: Indicators of military prerogatives beyond civilian purview — tutelary roles, constitutional mandates, and veto powers over security policy.
  • Security sector reform (SSR): Legislative, doctrinal, and organizational changes to armed forces — including DDR programs, defense white papers, and interoperability agreements.
  • Democratic backsliding: Patterns of institutional erosion — including the use of military force against domestic opponents, suspension of constitutional guarantees, and concentration of executive power via security decrees.
  • U.S. security cooperation: Channels and conduits of U.S. military engagement — FMF, IMET, SOUTHCOM exercises, equipment transfers, and joint operations — and their effects on recipient civil-military relations.
  • Transnational organized crime: The intersection of cartel, gang, and criminal network activity with state security forces — including counter-narcotics deployments, criminal infiltration of military and police units, illicit economy rents (drugs, mining, extortion), and state capture dynamics. SENTINEL treats military roles in the drug economy as core CMR phenomena, not peripheral security events.
Data Pipeline & Methodology

SENTINEL runs as a staged workflow. Source material is ingested from curated feeds and structured data providers, normalized into article-level records, screened for relevance, and then assembled into canonical event records. Duplicate detection, actor coding, QA, and publication checks are handled as distinct steps rather than being collapsed into one monolithic script.

Each event carries a provenance trail that records how it entered the system, what AI layers touched it, what supporting reports were linked to it, and whether it was later reviewed by a human. Low-confidence events can be withheld pending corroboration, while higher-confidence events can still be published and monitored publicly.

AI-generated council analysis now runs across all events and is explicitly labeled as AI-generated analysis. Human analysts do not manually code the full dataset from scratch; instead, they intervene on uncertainty, duplicates, publication-sensitive cases, and corrections.
